Methodology, honestly

How these projections are made

Every number on this site comes from models that predict raw stat lines — yards, touchdowns, receptions — never fantasy points directly. Points are computed from those stat lines under your scoring rules, so PPR, half-PPR and standard all come from one prediction.

The headline model is a small quantile transformer (PyTorch) that reads each player's recent game log and the matchup, and predicts a floor (p10), median (p50) and ceiling (p90) for every stat. The range bands you see on the weekly page start from those raw quantiles, then get calibrated per position — widened or narrowed so measured coverage tracks the 0.80 target — before they reach the site. Season bands on the draft board build on that calibrated weekly band through a season-long simulation (more on that below); uncertainty is the product, not an afterthought. The deployed projections average three independently seeded training runs of that model (a small ensemble), and the bake-off table below scores exactly that ensemble. Classical baselines (a naive last-four-games average and XGBoost) run through the exact same evaluation harness, and the tables below report whichever model wins, exactly as it lands.

Evaluation is walk-forward only: train on seasons up to S, test on season S+1, held out across 2023–2025. No random splits — rolling features would leak the future into the past.

Known limitations: rookies with no NFL games are not projected; players who changed teams keep their personal history with updated matchup context; retired players may appear until rosters are pruned; season projections seed every week from end-of-prior-season form, so a short late-season role spike (an injury fill-in) can be over-extrapolated across a full year while a star's slow finish under-ranks them — expect the board to have loud recency opinions; weekly bands are calibrated per position so roughly 80% of actual player-weeks land inside them, measured on 2023–2025 held-out seasons; season bands come from a Monte-Carlo simulation — games played sampled from historical position availability, weeks linked by a measured week-to-week correlation — and measured season coverage runs about 0.73–0.77 by position, narrower than the 0.80 target for QB and RB, and we're saying so plainly rather than rounding up; snap-count data has no 2012 source and is treated as missing for that season. These are design choices, documented, not surprises.
